# A German Court Just Ruled That AI Hallucinations Are Google's Problem — Not Yours

**June 10, 2026** | By Nine Under Places

---

## What

The Regional Court of Munich has issued a landmark ruling that rewrites the liability landscape for AI-generated content. In a temporary injunction (case no. 26 O 869/26), the court declared that Google's AI Overviews are **Google's own speech** — not third-party content — and that Google bears **direct legal responsibility** for false claims the AI generates. The ruling bars Google from spreading false statements about two Munich-based publishers.

The case began when Google's AI Overviews falsely linked two legitimate publishing companies to scams, subscription traps, and "shady business practices." The AI had mixed up information about genuinely fraudulent companies with the plaintiffs, drawing connections that appeared nowhere in any linked source document. The publishers sent a cease-and-desist letter. Google didn't respond adequately. The court acted.

The core legal reasoning is devastating for the "AI as neutral tool" defense. The court found that AI Overviews work nothing like traditional search results. The AI "rewrites and judges results in its own words and according to its own structure," confidently opening with claims like "Yes, [company] is known for dubious business practices" — then building its own architecture of summary, red flags, and user tips. Crucially, the court identified claims "that are not even made in the search results." These were Google's own fabrications.

## Why

This isn't just a German procedural quirk. It's a direct assault on the liability firewall the tech industry has been building around generative AI.

**The search engine shield is broken.** Germany's Federal Court of Justice (BGH) had previously established that search engines are only indirectly liable for results — they merely make third-party content findable. The Munich court explicitly rejected extending this reasoning to AI Overviews. "A regular search engine just points to outside websites. But AI overviews generate independent, new, and substantive statements by evaluating and combining content from various third-party sites." Different technology, different rules.

**Google's own defense backfired.** At the hearing, Google argued users could verify claims by checking linked sources themselves, claiming people "know that information generated with AI should not be blindly trusted." The court wasn't buying it. AI Overviews present themselves as authoritative answers, not caveated suggestions. And critically, the connections between sources and generated content often don't exist — users can't verify claims that the AI invented whole cloth.

**The scale argument cuts both ways.** Google cited studies showing their factuality rate is "over 90%." The court did the math: at billions of queries per day, even 91% accuracy means millions of wrong answers served daily. Scale amplifies liability, it doesn't dilute it.

**Eighty percent legal costs.** Google was ordered to pay 80% of the legal tab. That's not symbolic — it's calibrated to signal that this defense was substantially meritless.

The ruling also made a subtle but potent distinction: AI-generated content receives less free speech protection than human editorial content. When machines produce speech, the traditional deference to human expression doesn't apply in the same way.

## Impact

This ruling is the first crack in the dam — and the water is already coming through.

**Immediate EU cascading effects.** German courts rarely act in isolation on tech regulation. The EU's AI Act is already on the books. This ruling provides a concrete judicial template for how "AI-generated content" maps onto existing liability law. Expect similar cases in France, the Netherlands, and at the EU Court of Justice level within months.

**Business model reckoning.** If AI overviews are Google's own speech, every false claim becomes a potential lawsuit. Google serves billions of AI Overviews daily. Even at 99% accuracy — far beyond current capabilities — the absolute number of false statements is staggering. The economics of "ship first, fix later" just collided with European tort law.

**The precedent is portable.** The ruling's logic doesn't depend on Google specifically. Any company that generates content through AI — chatbots, summarizers, assistant responses — faces the same question: is this your speech, or are you a conduit? The Munich court's answer is clear: if you built the AI and control the algorithms, you own the output.

**Accuracy becomes a legal requirement.** The court explicitly compared AI Overviews to editorial content and found the technology wanting. If AI systems are treated as publishers rather than platforms, the entire design paradigm shifts. "Good enough" hallucination rates that are acceptable for search become legally indefensible for publishing.

For developers building AI products: the era of "AI-generated content may be inaccurate" disclaimers as a liability shield may be ending. If a German court can look past Google's warnings to the actual user experience — an AI speaking with confident authority — so can courts everywhere.
