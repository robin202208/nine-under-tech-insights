# China's Indium Phosphide Export Controls Are Choking the AI Data Center Buildout

**Date:** 2026-06-14  
**Category:** AI Hardware · Supply Chain · Geopolitics  
**Source:** Reuters / Mining Weekly

---

## What Happened

China's export restrictions on **indium phosphide (InP)** — imposed in February 2025 — have become the most acute materials bottleneck in the global AI data center supply chain. And the numbers tell a stark story: the average price of a 6-inch InP wafer has surged **250% to $5,000**. Lumentum, a major US photonics maker, is sold out through 2028 despite quadrupling output. AXT, the world's second-largest InP substrate producer, told investors that "InP export permits represent the most significant challenge we currently face."

The urgency reached the highest levels of government in May 2026. Barely a week after Nvidia-backed chipmaker Coherent warned of an InP shortage on an earnings call, its CEO Jim Anderson boarded a plane with President Trump's business delegation to China — partly to address export license delays. The issue was also on the agenda during US-China trade talks in Seoul ahead of the Trump-Xi summit.

Why does a niche semiconductor compound matter this much? Because AI data centers are hitting the physics ceiling of copper interconnects. The industry is pivoting to **photonics** — using light through optical fibers instead of electrical signals through copper — and InP is the irreplaceable core material for the lasers and modulators in 800G and 1.6T optical transceivers. As SemiAnalysis analyst Konrad Wang put it: "InP is one of several supply chain bottlenecks collectively gating AI data center buildouts."

China produces roughly **70% of the world's indium** and dominates the InP substrate manufacturing chain. The two global leaders — AXT (manufacturing in China) and Japan's Sumitomo Electric — together control nearly 80% of global InP substrate production. The restrictions ripple through the entire optical supply chain: Taiwanese manufacturers VPEC and LandMark Optoelectronics have faced substrate disruptions from AXT permit delays, while at least two major US photonics chipmakers have approached industry organizations for help with export licenses.

## Why It Matters

This isn't just another trade dispute. It's a structural supply bottleneck with a 2-3 year resolution timeline, landing precisely when the AI industry is trying to scale from tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands of GPUs per cluster.

The physics driving this is unforgiving. Copper interconnects hit bandwidth-distance limits that make them impractical for the scale of next-generation AI clusters. Photonics — specifically InP-based optical transceivers — is the only viable path for connecting 100K+ GPUs with acceptable latency and power. Nvidia clearly sees this: it announced $2 billion investments each into Coherent and Lumentum in March 2026, while Marvell Technology acquired photonics startup Celestial AI.

But building new InP substrate capacity doesn't happen overnight. Coherent is doubling its Texas InP wafer capacity this year and plans to more than double it again by end of 2027. Sumitomo is expanding. Chinese domestic players — Yunnan Germanium, Guangdong Xiandao, Zhuhai Dingtai Xinyuan — are rapidly scaling, with Yunnan Germanium reporting a 74% surge in InP wafer shipments. But new plants take 2-3 years to come online, and supplier qualification cycles for photonics chips add additional months.

Paul Triolo of Albright Stonebridge Group captured the strategic sophistication of Beijing's approach: "Rather than blocking finished photonics products outright, it can slow or condition the export of the upstream compounds, substrates, metals... that determine whether the optical-module ecosystem can scale quickly enough to meet hyperscaler demand." This "materials chokepoint" strategy is more refined than China's earlier rare earths play — it targets a specific, high-value, no-substitute node in a rapidly growing supply chain.

## The Bigger Picture

The InP bottleneck exposes a structural vulnerability in the AI infrastructure buildout that market forces alone cannot solve quickly. Every major AI lab — OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, xAI — is scaling to larger clusters. Every hyperscaler is racing to deploy optical interconnects. But they all depend on a supply chain where one country controls the critical upstream material and can throttle exports at will.

There are workarounds in progress. Coherent's Texas expansion, Sumitomo's capacity additions, and potential non-Chinese substrate sources could eventually diversify supply. But the 2-3 year gap between now and sufficient capacity means the AI industry faces a real risk of data center buildouts being constrained not by capital, demand, or even GPU availability — but by the inability to get enough optical transceivers to connect the GPUs they already have.

The market signal is also clear: semiconductor nationalism is expanding from logic chips (TSMC, ASML) into compound semiconductors and specialty materials. The playbook China developed with rare earths is being applied to the AI supply chain with surgical precision, and the industry has no quick answer.
