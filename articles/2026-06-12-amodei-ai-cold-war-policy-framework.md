# Anthropic's CEO Just Proposed Treating AI Models Like Aircraft — With the FAA as the Model

> Dario Amodei's new essay "Policy on the AI Exponential" calls for mandatory government audits of frontier AI, the power to block dangerous models, and a Cold War-style alliance of democracies walling off adversaries through supply chains and export controls. It's the most detailed governance proposal yet from a frontier lab CEO.

## What Happened

On June 11, Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei published a sweeping essay alongside two policy frameworks: one for regulating frontier AI and one for managing AI-driven job displacement. The essay opens with a Lord of the Rings metaphor — Treebeard the Ent is wise but so slow he can't act against the approaching army in time. The slow tree creature is the political system. The approaching army is unregulated AI.

Amodei's thesis: the transparency-only approach Anthropic previously advocated is no longer sufficient. The evidence is Claude Mythos Preview, which "disrupted the global cybersecurity landscape" and demonstrated that frontier models carry real risks for financial systems, critical infrastructure, and national security. He expects biological risks and serious autonomy risks to follow "sooner rather than later."

The core proposal is modeled on the FAA. Just as aircraft must pass technical inspections before they fly, frontier AI models should undergo mandatory safety testing by qualified third parties before deployment. A government agency should have the authority to block or pull models that pose unacceptable risk across four categories: cybersecurity, biological weapons, loss of control, and automated R&D that accelerates these risks.

## The Framework's Architecture

The Advanced AI Framework is aimed primarily at the US federal government. Rather than covering the entire industry, it targets the top tier: developers training models above 10²⁵ FLOP who either generate over $500 million in AI revenue or spend more than $1 billion annually on AI R&D. Anthropic proposes reviewing these thresholds annually and eventually shifting from raw compute criteria to capability-based ones — since the compute needed for dangerous capability will drop over time.

Covered developers must publish a safety framework, provide system cards for high-risk models, file risk reports at least every six months, report serious security incidents within 15 days, and hire at least one independent evaluator with no financial ties to the company within six months. To prevent "evaluator shopping," Anthropic proposes a government rating and assignment system for auditors — you don't pick your inspector, the regulator assigns one.

On federal preemption, Anthropic takes a careful position: Congress should only override state law if it creates a federal regime that's at least as strict. This is calibrated to prevent weaker federal rules from gutting California's SB 53 and similar state-level protections.

## The Economic Framework: Tiered Response to Mass Displacement

The jobs framework scales measures to unemployment severity:

**Tier 1 (~5% unemployment):** Universal capital accounts created at birth, wage insurance, occupational licensing reform, training subsidies.

**Tier 2 (~10%):** Expanded unemployment insurance, basic needs assistance.

**Tier 3 (exceeding historic highs):** Universal basic income, AI sovereign wealth funds, higher capital gains taxes. Anthropic explicitly states it's willing to pay its "fair share" if AI companies generate transformative profits.

## The Geopolitical Lens

The worldview is unmistakably Cold War in structure. Amodei frames AI not as a consumer technology but in the same category as nuclear weapons — something that reshapes the entire geopolitical playing field. A nation with advanced AI facing one without it would be like "a Marine force against medieval swordsmen." He factors in fully autonomous drone armies.

Cooperation is framed as an alliance of like-minded democracies, walling themselves off from autocracies through shared supply chains and tightened chip export controls. It's containment strategy applied to compute.

## Why It Matters

Anthropic is simultaneously filing for IPO, shipping its first public Mythos-class model (Claude Fable 5), and proposing the most aggressive regulatory framework any frontier lab has put forward. The tension is real but not necessarily contradictory: Amodei is arguing that the industry should accept binding constraints before disaster forces them, and that early constraints are better than later, harsher ones imposed reactively.

The FAA analogy is the conceptual anchor. It reframes AI safety from an abstract philosophical debate into an engineering discipline with inspection checkpoints. Whether Congress adopts any of this is uncertain — the 269-page Great American AI Act introduced earlier this month already shows movement in the regulatory direction Amodei is pushing. The question is whether the speed of legislation can match the speed of capability scaling.

The essay also signals Anthropic's positioning ahead of its IPO. It says: we're the responsible frontier lab, we want to be regulated, and if there's going to be a government-enforced moat around AI development, we're ready to live inside it. Whether that positioning is principled, strategic, or both depends on your priors.

## Developer Impact

For developers building AI systems, the practical implication is clear: if Amodei's framework gets traction, safety testing becomes a gate — not an afterthought. Models won't ship until they pass third-party audits for cybersecurity risk, bio-risk, loss-of-control risk, and automated R&D risk. System cards, incident reporting, and independent evaluators become mandatory infrastructure.

The compute thresholds (10²⁵ FLOP) put this regulation at the frontier tier for now, but the proposed shift to capability-based criteria means the bar lowers as efficiency improves. A model that's dangerous at 10²⁴ FLOP in 2028 would be caught by capability-based gates even though it's under the compute floor.

The economic framework's tiered UBI proposal matters too: if Anthropic is willing to fund it, other labs will face pressure to follow. The era of AI companies building without accounting for labor displacement costs may be ending.
