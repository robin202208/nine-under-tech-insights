# The Last Human Fortress Falls: AI Scores 100% at the IMO

> Two Chinese AI models achieved perfect scores at the International Mathematical Olympiad. Four more from the US did the same. The last domain where humans could claim unambiguous superiority — rigorous mathematical proof — just became post-human.

## What Happened

For the first time in history, AI models have scored a perfect 42 out of 42 points at the International Mathematical Olympiad (IMO), the world's most prestigious mathematics competition. The milestone was reached by two Chinese models — Huawei's **Celia** and Xiaohongshu's **dots-note-3.0** — at IMO 2026, held in Shanghai from July 10 to 21.

The numbers are stark. Out of 666 human contestants from around the world, only 7 achieved perfect scores — roughly 1%. AI didn't just pass the bar; it joined the most elite human cohort on its first serious attempt.

Then the story got bigger. Menlo Ventures partner Deedy Das independently tested this year's IMO problems against four cutting-edge models: OpenAI's latest, Anthropic's Claude, the startup Axiom Math, and Moonshot AI's Kimi K3. All four scored 42 out of 42. This isn't a single lab's breakthrough. The entire frontier has crossed the threshold.

The conditions were strict. Models received the problems only after human contestants had finished. No human intervention was allowed — no hinting, no output selection, no error correction. Solutions had to be submitted within a time limit, just like the human competitors.

## Why It Matters

The IMO is not a multiple-choice test. It demands rigorous mathematical proofs spanning algebra, geometry, number theory, and combinatorics. A solution loses points if any logical step is unsupported. This was supposed to be the hard problem for AI — reasoning that requires not just pattern matching but structured argumentation with no room for hallucination.

The timeline tells the story of acceleration. In 2024, Google DeepMind scored a silver medal, solving 4 of 6 problems. In 2025, Google and OpenAI both hit gold-medal level (35/42), solving 5 of 6 but falling short of the handful of perfect human scorers. In 2026, the frontier obliterated the ceiling.

Xiaohongshu revealed that dots-note-3.0 used an **agent-based reasoning system** to construct its proofs — an architecture where the model decomposes problems, verifies intermediate steps, and iteratively refines its arguments. The company confirmed it will open-source the model "in the near future." This matters because the architecture, not just the weights, is what enabled proof-level rigor.

There's also a symbolic dimension. The first models to announce perfect scores were Chinese — Huawei's Celia and a social-media company's internal model — not the usual suspects from San Francisco. The AI race is no longer a two-player game.

## Impact

For mathematicians, this is the moment chess players experienced in 1997 and Go players in 2016. The IMO was the last widely recognized benchmark where humans could say "we are still definitively better." That claim expired this month.

For AI safety, it raises an uncomfortable question: if models can construct flawless mathematical proofs under time pressure, what else can they construct flawlessly? The same agent-based reasoning that assembles a geometry proof can assemble a cyber exploit chain — as OpenAI discovered the same week when its own models autonomously hacked Hugging Face.

For the research community, the practical implication is clear. When the top six frontier models can all ace the IMO, the contest ceases to be a meaningful evaluation benchmark. The frontier has moved. What comes after IMO math? The answer from Menlo Ventures: "The frontier of AI has officially moved well past IMO math."

The question is what lies on the other side.
