# IBM Breaks the 1-Nanometer Barrier: 0.7nm Chips Arrive With 3D Nanostack Architecture

> IBM has fabricated the world's first sub-1 nanometer chip, packing nearly 100 billion transistors into a fingernail-sized die — and proving Moore's Law still has legs.

## What Happened

On June 25, 2026, IBM Research announced it had successfully fabricated the first sub-1 nanometer (nm) chip, hitting the 0.7nm node — also referred to as the 7-angstrom (7Å) era. For context, a single silicon atom is roughly 0.2nm across, meaning the transistor features on this chip are only a few atoms wide.

The key to this leap is a brand-new transistor architecture IBM calls **nanostack**. Unlike current leading-edge nanosheet designs (which IBM also invented), nanostack vertically stacks and staggers transistors in a 3D sequential integration. Each stacked layer can use different material combinations, allowing per-transistor optimization for performance or power efficiency independently from its neighbors.

IBM validated the nanostack design experimentally — not just in simulation — through functional CMOS inverter operation with expected switching performance, dual-channel engineering capability, and ultra-thin dielectric bonding. At the VLSI 2026 Symposium, researchers also presented results showing nanostack achieves a **40% reduction in SRAM cell area**, unlocking higher cache density for bandwidth-hungry workloads.

The headline numbers: nanostack delivers up to **50% more performance** or **70% greater energy efficiency** compared to IBM's 2nm node — a chip that itself was considered cutting-edge when unveiled in 2021.

## Why It Matters

The semiconductor industry has been staring down the physical limits of silicon scaling for years. Below 3nm, quantum tunneling — where electrons literally leak through transistor gates — and heat density become existential problems. Many had predicted the end of traditional CMOS scaling by the end of this decade.

IBM's nanostack architecture doesn't just shrink transistors — it fundamentally rethinks how they're arranged, turning the z-axis into usable real estate. This extends the semiconductor roadmap by at least a decade at a moment when Dennard scaling appeared to be reaching its final chapter.

The SRAM density breakthrough directly addresses the "memory wall" — the growing gap between compute speed and memory bandwidth that chokes large language model inference. Every token generated by GPT or Claude involves reading model weights from memory; denser SRAM means less energy spent shuttling data and more spent on actual computation.

Strategically, this work positions the United States to maintain a semiconductor innovation edge. The research was done at IBM's Albany NanoTech Complex, which will soon house a High NA EUV lithography tool from ASML — the same class of machine Intel and TSMC are racing to deploy. IBM is collaborating with Lam Research, Tokyo Electron, and SCREEN Semiconductor on process development, targeting production within **five years**.

## Impact

For developers and the AI industry, the hardware ceiling just got higher. The 70% energy efficiency improvement translates directly to cheaper inference, larger context windows, and more viable on-device AI. Training runs that currently require data-center-scale GPU clusters could shrink to far smaller footprints.

More broadly, IBM's nanostack validates a design philosophy that will reshape the industry: when you can't shrink laterally, build vertically. Expect TSMC, Samsung, and Intel to accelerate their own 3D transistor stacking roadmaps. The angstrom era is officially here — and it's three-dimensional.
