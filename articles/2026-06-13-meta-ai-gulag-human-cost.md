# Meta Drafted 6,500 Engineers Into Its AI Division. They're Calling It a Gulag.

> A Wired investigation reveals Meta's three-month-old Applied AI unit — where engineers were forcibly reassigned to generate training puzzles under "join or quit" terms — is on the verge of revolt. The episode exposes a structural tension the AI industry hasn't acknowledged: when your training data requires human intelligence, your most talented employees become your most expensive input cost.

## What Happened

In March 2026, roughly 6,500 Meta engineers and product managers received a surprise email informing them they'd been "drafted" into a new organization: Applied AI. There was no application process, no manager conversation, no opt-out. The terms, per multiple reports, were join or quit.

Their assignment: generate puzzles, coding problems, and task demonstrations to train Meta's AI models. The stated rationale, according to an internal announcement reviewed by Business Insider, was that "for agents to understand how people actually complete everyday tasks using computers, we need to train our models on real examples." In a leaked internal meeting, CEO Mark Zuckerberg offered a blunter explanation — Scale AI founder Alexandr Wang (now Meta's chief AI officer) "knows the data-labeling world well," and the average Meta employee has "significantly higher" intelligence than third-party contractors.

This week, the situation boiled over. During a livestreamed internal presentation, someone hijacked the feed with an expletive-laden outburst demanding attendees tell a senior AI executive he was "a piece of sh*t." One presenter reportedly covered their face with their hands. A Wired investigation published June 12 quotes engineers describing the unit as "literally the gulag" and the work as "soul-crushing."

The unit is led by Maher Saba, a 12-year Meta veteran previously a VP in Reality Labs — the division that burned through $83 billion on the metaverse before Meta pivoted hard to AI. The original structure had up to 50 employees reporting to a single manager. It reports up to CTO Andrew Bosworth.

Separately, over 1,600 Meta employees have signed a petition protesting a program that monitors their clicks and keystrokes to collect AI training data. Zuckerberg issued a Friday memo acknowledging the company had "caused distress" and made mistakes it would address.

## Why It Matters

This isn't a workplace culture story. It's a structural economics story.

AI training has a bottleneck that most of the industry doesn't talk about: for certain capabilities — reasoning, tool use, agentic behavior — you need data generated by highly intelligent humans. You can scrape the internet for text. You can't scrape it for the internal thought process of a senior engineer debugging a distributed system.

Meta's move makes brutal economic sense. Why pay contractors when you already employ 6,500 people earning $300K+ who can be reassigned at zero marginal hiring cost? The cost of replacing them (recruiting, onboarding, institutional knowledge loss) is borne by them, not the company, in a labor market where AI engineers are simultaneously in high demand and terrified of being automated.

The deeper tension: the AI industry is building systems designed to replace knowledge workers, using knowledge workers who are increasingly aware they're training their own replacements. Meta has laid off tens of thousands while funneling billions into AI infrastructure. The Applied AI engineers aren't just doing boring work — they're acutely conscious that every puzzle they generate makes the models better at tasks they used to be paid to do.

Zuckerberg's "higher intelligence" comment is the tell. He wasn't being arrogant. He was stating a production constraint: the models need smart inputs, and smart inputs are expensive. Drafting your own employees is the logical solution to that constraint — if you ignore the human cost.

## Developer Impact

Every AI company building agentic systems faces the same problem Meta just solved with brute force: **where do you get training data for tasks that require real intelligence?** The answer, for now, is "from smart humans." Meta chose its own employees. Other companies will choose different populations — contractors, gig workers, users — but the economic logic is the same.

For individual engineers, the Meta episode sharpens a question that was already uncomfortable: if your daily work becomes training data for an AI that can eventually do your job, what exactly are you building? The Applied AI engineers have answered that question, and they're calling it a gulag.

For AI leaders, the Meta case is a cautionary tale about organizational design in the age of AI. You can draft talent. You can't draft morale. The 1,600-employee keystroke-monitoring petition suggests the backlash isn't limited to one unit — it's company-wide. When your AI strategy depends on extracting intelligence from employees who see themselves as raw material, the revolt isn't a bug. It's the feature you designed.

## Our Take

Meta's "gulag" isn't an outlier — it's an early warning. Every AI lab that needs human-generated reasoning data will face the same structural pressure. The difference is execution. Zuckerberg approached it like a logistics problem (reallocate headcount, maximize intelligence-per-dollar) and got a logistics outcome: bodies in seats, burning out. The companies that win will be the ones that treat their engineers as partners in building AI, not as a temporarily useful training corpus.
